Talent Identification Insights
Birth quarter distribution, maturation status, and how key metrics develop across age groups
⚡ Relative Age Effect — Birth Quarter Distribution
In youth sport, players born earlier in the calendar year are physically older than peers in the same cohort and are systematically over-selected. Across all PDP age groups, Q1-born players (Jan–Mar) are significantly overrepresented — often comprising 30–45% of a group where 25% would be expected if there were no bias.

What to watch for: A Q4-born player with strong Copa, Technical, or Cognitive scores is outperforming despite a structural calendar disadvantage. Their ceiling is likely higher than their current numbers show. Conversely, a Q1-born player with only average scores may be benefiting from temporary physical advantages that will erode over time.
📈 How Key Metrics Develop Across Age Groups
The charts below show average performance by age group (Boys in blue, Girls in pink). Use these to understand expected improvement curves and to identify athletes who are outperforming their age group norms.
🏃 Top Speed
Speed develops steadily with age and maturation. Boys show steeper gains (+0.5–0.8 mph per group) while girls improve more gradually. By U17, boys average ~3 mph faster than U12 boys — a meaningful athletic gap that scouts should account for when projecting younger players.
🧠 VTS Stress Tolerance
Cognitive stress tolerance improves with age but remains highly variable, particularly at U12 and U13. By U16/U17 it becomes one of the most differentiating metrics — elite performers at older ages score significantly above peers. High stress tolerance at a young age is a strong talent signal.
⚡ Cube Speed
Athletes get faster at the cube drill as they age. The steepest improvements come between U12 and U14, after which gains plateau. Lower is better — a U17 elite performer completes the drill significantly faster than the average U12. A young athlete with already-fast cube speed has a high ceiling.
🎯 Cube Accuracy
Accuracy improves modestly with age but is one of the highest-variance metrics at every level. Top performers stand out strongly at all ages. A U13 with top-decile cube accuracy is a notable outlier worth tracking — they are showing technical precision well ahead of their cohort.
🏆 Cube Rating
The composite cube score climbs steadily with age. The gap between the average and the best performers widens at U16/U17 — elite performers become increasingly distinct from their peers. A young player already near the top of their age group cube rating is on a strong development trajectory.
🦴 Maturation Status Breakdown
Skeletal age is measured independently of calendar age. An Early maturer has a skeletal age more than 1 year ahead of calendar age — they may appear physically dominant now but can plateau as peers catch up. A Late maturer is biologically younger than their peers — their best athletic development may still be ahead of them. On-Time players are developing in line with their chronological age.

High-upside scouting profile: Late maturer + Q4 born + elite Technical/Cognitive scores = the most valuable profile in this dataset. This player is outperforming despite two structural disadvantages, and their physical development has not yet peaked.
Dashboard Methodology
How this dashboard is built, how to read it, and what to look for as a scout or coach
How Percentile Rankings Work

Every score in a player's profile is ranked against all other players in the same age group and gender who were assessed in the same assessment cohort. This means a U13 Girl's Copa Score percentile compares her only to other U13 Girls — not to older or younger players, and not across genders.

100th percentile = best in that age group/gender. 0th percentile = lowest. All metrics are scaled this way — even metrics where a lower raw number is better (like Agility time or VTS MRT). A player ranked 94th percentile for Agility ran the fastest, not the slowest.

For players with multiple assessments, each assessment's percentile is calculated against the cohort at the time of that assessment. So if a player was assessed as U13 and again as U14, their U13 percentile reflects where they stood among U13s, and their U14 percentile reflects U14s.

Talent Identification Insights

The following insights are derived from the full PDP cohort and are intended to help scouts and coaches contextualize what they see in a player's profile.

Relative Age Effect (RAE): Across all age groups, players born in Q1 (January–March) are significantly overrepresented. This is a well-documented bias in youth sport — older players within a year cohort appear more developed and are more likely to be selected. When you see a Q4 player performing at an elite level, they are doing so against a structural disadvantage.
Maturation Status: A player listed as "Early" maturer has a skeletal age more than 1 year ahead of their calendar age. They may appear physically dominant now but could plateau relative to later developers. "Late" maturers are biologically younger than their peers — their best athletic development may still be ahead of them. "On-Time" players are developing in line with their chronological age.
Skeletal Age vs Calendar Age: Girls tend to mature earlier — skeletal age runs ahead of calendar age at every group through U16. Boys stay closer to on-time through U15, then converge. By U17, both genders show slight skeletal-age lag, suggesting the most advanced maturers have plateaued relative to their peers.
What to watch for: A Q4-born, late-maturer with elite cognitive and technical scores is a high-upside prospect — they are excelling despite structural and biological disadvantage. A Q1-born early-maturer with only average scores may be benefiting from physical advantages that diminish over time.
